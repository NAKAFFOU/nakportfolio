Based on your reading of the case study(Kovaitė and Stankevičienė, 2019) answer the following questions in the discussion forum:
 - What do the authors mean by the term ‘Industry 4.0’ - give two examples.
 - Give two real-world examples of risks that fit into the authors categories.
 - Find another journal article that either supports or contradicts the points made in the cited study.

Initial Post - By Nelson Akaffou - Monday, 5 August 2024, 7:35 PM

by Nelson Akaffou - Monday, 5 August 2024, 7:35 

Industry 4.0 refers to the fourth industrial revolution, which is characterized by the integration of digital technologies into manufacturing and industrial processes. This revolution encompasses a range of technological advancements that enable smart and autonomous systems fuelled by data and machine learning. The primary goal of Industry 4.0 is to create interconnected and intelligent production environments that enhance efficiency, flexibility, and productivity.Two Examples of Industry 4.0:Internet of Things (IoT):Industry 4.0 has been considered a new industrial stage in which several emerging or disruptive technologies including Internet of Things (IoT), artificial intelligence (AI), 3D printing, and big data are converging to provide digital solutions (Franck & Ayala, 2019).Robotics and Automation:The advent of the fourth industrial revolution (Industry 4.0) is paving the way for new ways to automate mundane rules-based business processes, using RPA tools on information obtained from smart devices (Madakam et al, 2019).When discussing risks in the context of Industry 4.0, authors typically categorize them into several types, such as technical, competence, behavioural, data security risks and financial risks. Here are two real-world examples that fit into these categories:Data security Risk:Example: In 2017, the WannaCry ransomware attack targeted numerous organizations worldwide, including manufacturing plants.WannaCry Ransomware Attack 2017 was the worst attack that ever had before. WannaCry Ransomware is a type of malicious software that blocks user access to files or systems, holding files or entire devices hostage using encryption until the victim pays a ransom in exchange for a decryption key, which allows the user to access the files or systems encrypted by the program (Algarni,2021).Technical Risk:Example: In 2019, Boeing faced significant operational risks with the grounding of its 737 Max aircraft following two fatal crashes. The crashes were linked to software issues in the Manoeuvring Characteristics Augmentation System (MCAS). The CVR and FDR data directly indicate that the MCAS might have been the core-reason of the incidents. The brand-new B-737 Max aircraft were flying with the new type of engines in the name of fuel consumption efficiency (Saraçyakupoğlu, 2020). An article by Ibarra et al (2018) offers a somewhat contrasting perspective. It emphasizes the opportunities and benefits that Industry 4.0 brings to business model innovation, especially for SMEs. Their review highlights how digital transformation can lead to more efficient processes, enhanced customer experiences, and new revenue streams, despite acknowledging the inherent risks (Inderscience). These two perspectives together illustrate the complexity of Industry 4.0, where the potential for innovation and efficiency is significant, but so are the risks that need to be carefully managed.
