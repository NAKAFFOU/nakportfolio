 - What do the authors mean by the term ‘Industry 4.0’ - give two examples.
 - Give two real-world examples of risks that fit into the authors categories.
 - Find another journal article that either supports or contradicts the points made in the cited study.

Initial Post by Nelson Akaffou - Monday,5 August 2024,7:35 PM

Industry 4.0 refers to the fourth industrial revolution, which is characterized by the integration of digital technologies into manufacturing and industrial processes. This revolution encompasses a range of technological advancements that enable smart and autonomous systems fueled by data and machine learning. The primary goal of Industry 4.0 is to create interconnected and intelligent production environments that enhance efficiency, flexibility, and productivity.

Two Examples of Industry 4.0:

Internet of Things (IoT):
Industry 4.0 has been considered a new industrial stage in which several emerging or disruptive technologies including Internet of Things (IoT), artificial intelligence (AI), 3D printing, and big data are converging to provide digital solutions (Franck & Ayala, 2019).

Robotics and Automation:
The advent of the fourth industrial revolution (Industry 4.0) is paving the way for new ways to automate mundane rules-based business processes, using RPA tools on information obtained from smart devices (Madakam et al., 2019).

When discussing risks in the context of Industry 4.0, authors typically categorize them into several types, such as technical, competence, behavioral, data security risks, and financial risks. Here are two real-world examples that fit into these categories:

Data Security Risk:
Example: In 2017, the WannaCry ransomware attack targeted numerous organizations worldwide, including manufacturing plants. WannaCry Ransomware Attack 2017 was the worst attack that ever had before. WannaCry Ransomware is a type of malicious software that blocks user access to files or systems, holding files or entire devices hostage using encryption until the victim pays a ransom in exchange for a decryption key, which allows the user to access the files or systems encrypted by the program (Algarni, 2021).

Technical Risk:
Example: In 2019, Boeing faced significant operational risks with the grounding of its 737 Max aircraft following two fatal crashes. The crashes were linked to software issues in the Maneuvering Characteristics Augmentation System (MCAS). The CVR and FDR data directly indicate that the MCAS might have been the core reason for the incidents. The brand-new B-737 Max aircraft were flying with the new type of engines in the name of fuel consumption efficiency (Saraçyakupoğlu, 2020). An article by Ibarra et al. (2018) offers a somewhat contrasting perspective. It emphasizes the opportunities and benefits that Industry 4.0 brings to business model innovation, especially for SMEs. Their review highlights how digital transformation can lead to more efficient processes, enhanced customer experiences, and new revenue streams, despite acknowledging the inherent risks (Inderscience).

These two perspectives together illustrate the complexity of Industry 4.0, where the potential for innovation and efficiency is significant, but so are the risks that need to be carefully managed.

Peer Response by Tobias Zeier - Thursday, 8 August 2024, 4:34 PM

Boeing 737 Max Case Analysis

Thank you, Nelson, for your post which discusses a critical event in the recent history of civil aviation.

I would argue that the Boeing 737 Max case was indeed a cascading effect of different issues. To be able to compete with Airbus' A320neo, Boeing had to come up with something new, since the A320neo was much more efficient compared to Boeing’s 737. Boeing made a conscious decision to use the same aircraft type and optimise the engines. The strategy was to keep the same airplane type, to avoid costly training for pilots. To make the 737 more efficient, the engines were enlarged but also had to be moved further forward so that they were still far enough away from the ground. As the aircraft behaved completely differently as a result, an attempt was made to compensate for this with software (MCAS) that corrected the aircraft's attitude by overriding the pilot. Consequently, the pilots were taken by surprise when the software suddenly intervened and changed the aircraft's behaviour (Gonela et al., 2020).

Therefore, I would even go so far and declare this catastrophe as a major failure in Boeing's leadership, since the management team prioritised financial aspects over safety. This fundamental error could not be corrected by software, and skipping flight simulator training did not help either.

Tutor Response by Doug Millward - Thursday, 8 August 2024, 6:03 PM

Hi Nelson & Tobi,

You both make some excellent points - but I was particularly interested in Tobi's view about the fact that the issue illustrates management failings rather than technical. This goes to the heart of the criticism of Industry 4.0 in that it focuses too much on technology and not enough on the people. As Tobi says, this was very much a management failing - cutting corners and telling operators that (costly) retraining was not needed - although the technology was changing to a large degree and additional training was absolutely required. The risk here was more about the human factor than the technical one - which led to costly reputational damage. As we say in the UK, this was an example of 'penny wise, pound foolish' - that is saving some money in retraining but losing much more in reputational damage and lost sales amongst other factors.

What do you both think?

Summary Post by Nelson Akaffou - Wednesday, 14 August 2024, 9:30 AM

Thank you, Tobi and Doug.

I think the point you made is totally correct. The Boeing 737 MAX crisis is a prime example of how systemic management failures can overshadow technical issues. While the MCAS (Manoeuvring Characteristics Augmentation System) software was a contributing factor to the two fatal crashes, it was the management failure that ultimately led to the disaster.

The key management failures are, according to me:
- Prioritizing Profit Over Safety: Boeing faced intense competition from Airbus. To maintain market share and profitability, the company opted for a quick and cost-effective redesign of the 737 rather than developing a new aircraft from scratch. This decision prioritized speed and cost over safety. “Competitive pressure from Airbus placed pressure on Boeing to respond with its own. The timeline was largely dictated by Airbus, not the time Boeing engineers needed to complete the project” (Johnston & Harris, 2019).
- Failure to Close the Training Gap: “Boeing claimed the 737 MAX was so like the original 737 that pilots already licensed for this aircraft would not need additional training and simulator time for the 737 MAX.” (Johnston & Harris, 2019). “While manufacturers such as Airbus and Boeing continue to modernize cockpits, there must be an emphasis on ensuring that new technology is properly understood by the aircrew communities, particularly its failure modes. Rigorous human factor assessments and training are also required from the manufacturers.” (Lengyel et al, 2023).
- Ethical Issues: After the first crash, Boeing was slow to acknowledge the severity of the problem and implement necessary changes. This delay contributed to the second fatal crash.

In summary, we agree that these perspectives illustrate that while Industry 4.0 offers substantial opportunities for innovation, it also necessitates careful consideration of both technological and human factors to avoid costly and dangerous mistakes.
